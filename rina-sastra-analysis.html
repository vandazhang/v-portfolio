Overview

This project simulates the kind of content performance analysis I ran regularly at Google (YouTube Music) and ByteDance (TikTok Music) — where reading the data and translating it into actionable recommendations was a core part of the role.

I built a realistic mock dataset for Rina Sastra, a fictional Indonesian indie artist, and used Python to clean, analyse, and visualise 3 months of social media posting activity. The goal was to answer one central question: where and how should Rina focus her content effort to grow fastest?

The answer, backed by the data: TikTok first, Music Clips always, and front-load every release week.

Key findings & recommendations

01
Go TikTok-first
5.4×

TikTok delivers 5.4× more reach and 3× higher engagement per post. Instagram should be maintained for aesthetics but TikTok is the primary growth engine. Posting ratio recommendation: 2 TikTok for every 1 Instagram post.

02
Lead with Music & BTS
10.6%

Music Clips and Behind the Scenes content consistently outperform promotional posts. Audiences connect more with the creative process than the sell. Limit pure promo posts to 1 per release week — let the music content do the announcing.

03
Front-load release weeks
+48%

Campaign periods show a clear and consistent engagement lift. Concentrate 3–4 posts in the days around each release drop to capitalise on the algorithm's momentum window, rather than spreading content evenly.

Methodology

Python pandas matplotlib seaborn Google Colab Google Sheets

A mock dataset of 32 posts was generated to simulate realistic social media performance for an emerging Indonesian indie artist across Q1 2024. Metrics — including reach, likes, comments, shares, saves, and engagement rate — were modelled on industry benchmarks for micro-influencer and indie artist accounts in the Southeast Asian market.

The dataset includes two simulated campaign windows (a February single release and a March collab drop) to test whether release-period activity produces a measurable signal in the data. It does.

Data was loaded into Google Colab, cleaned and transformed using pandas, and visualised using matplotlib and seaborn. All tools used are free and open source. The full annotated notebook is available to view on Google Colab.
